Clingmans Dome (Tennessee)

Elevation: 6,643ft / 2,025m
Date Completed: November 24, 2021
Chronology: High point #35 of 50

Figure 1: A section of the walkway to the Clingmans Dome observation tower, with the Great Smoky Mountains in the background.

Figure 2: Tom poses on top of the Clingmans Dome observation tower.

Clingmans Dome is the tallest mountain and the highest point in Great Smoky Mountains National Park, and in the state of Tennessee. Much like other Appalachian high points, there's a network of trails surrounding it, so you can pick and choose where you want to start from and how long you want your approach to be. I started from Newfound Gap, a viewpoint along route 441 that has a large parking lot, and is connected to Clingmans Dome via 7.9 miles of the Appalachian Trail. I managed to fill almost all available daylight with this climb. It took me seven and a half hours, from 8:30am to 4:00pm.

Figure 3: A sign explains that the area is fenced off from wild hogs in order to protect plants.

Figure 4: Incredible pig-proof technology, as seen on the Appalachian Trail near Newfound Gap.

Not too long after I got started, I came to a beech forest enclosure showcasing some pig-proof technology. A helpful sign explained that the area's plants are vulnerable to wild hogs. The solution is to build a fence that keeps the hogs out--the fence is on an incline, and an open grated platform is built horizontally, so as to allow a person to walk right over the fence. A hog, however, would likely be afraid to walk on the grated surface due to its smaller feet. I'm assuming a hog would think that way, but I haven't been one before, so I'm not entirely sure.

Figure 6: A mile-marking sign at the trailhead at Road Prong.

Since I started from so far away, I had to cross two foothills before climbing Clingmans Dome itself. This meant that I had a lot of elevation change, as I had to go up, down, up, down, and up again--then, the opposite way in reverse when going back to my car. Signs along the Appalachian Trail continually remind you of how far away you are.

Figure 7: The observation tower on top of Clingmans Dome.

Figure 8: Tom poses in front of the observation tower on top of Clingmans Dome.

At the summit of Clingmans Dome is an observation tower architected such that a spiral-shaped concrete walkway leads to the top of the tower. The summit was crowded when I got there, and I get the impression that's usually the case, but that it may have been extra crowded the day I was there since it was during the Thanksgiving weekend.

Figures 9-12: Views from the summit of Clingmans Dome.

The trail was mostly wooded, since the Appalachians aren't typically high enough to have a treeline. Certain points along the trail were very wooded, to the point that minimal sunlight entered the woods and it felt dark even during the day.

Because the trail was so wooded, there were not many scenic views along the way. One specific point, shown in Figures 15 and 16, was the only standout besides what I was able to see from the observation tower on the summit. The two photos are a side-by-side comparison of what the view looked like on the way up in the morning, and on the way down in the afternoon.
