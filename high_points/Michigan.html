Mt. Arvon (Michigan)

Elevation: 1,979ft / 603m
Date Completed: June 3, 2019
Chronology: High point #20 of 50

Figure 1: The High Point sign at the Mt. Arvon summit.

Figure 2: The Mt. Arvon register, the Highpointer's Bench, and a conveniently-placed bottle of Woodbridge at the Mt. Arvon summit.

Michigan's upper peninsula has always mystified me, and as I'm writing this I have to admit that it's funny now that I've visited it for this trip and yet I've still never been to mainland Michigan. But it's a really beautiful place.

Mt. Arvon, the state high point, is arguably not accessible without all-wheel drive capabilities. You can get to the summit if you have a sedan, but you may need to park many miles away and make it a long hike in. Ry and I had rented a Jeep to be able to drive as close to the mountain as possible on this trip. We parked along the Ravine River, and hiked the blue trail to the summit.

Figure 3: A portion of the marked blue trail to Mt. Arvon.

Figure 4: A different portion of the marked blue trail to Mt. Arvon.

The blue trail is marked by the trees, though there isn't much of a trail on the ground. We didn't have any problems following it, though. There are some nice waterfalls along the way. The woods in this area felt similar to the woods of my home state of Connecticut, especially in the summertime.

Figure 5: Trails near the summit of Mt. Arvon.

Figure 6: The view through a clearing from near the summit of Mt. Arvon.

At the summit, we found a mailbox register, an informative blue sign, and a High Pointer's Bench. There was a network of trails that roamed the hillside next to the summit, and which provided the greatest vista around.

The surrounding area, which harbored an active logging industry, had some marshy ponds and an array of colorful trees. It felt remote--even particularly among all of the high points I've been to. I'd like to go back some day, but I just don't know what would bring me there--such is the nature of the place.
