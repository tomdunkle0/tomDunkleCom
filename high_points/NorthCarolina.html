Mt. Mitchell (North Carolina)

Elevation: 6,684ft / 2,037m
Date Completed: May 19, 2023
Chronology: High point #41 of 50

Figure 1: Elisha Mitchell's grave at the summit of Mt. Mitchell.

Figure 2: Tom and Ry posing with the sign at the summit of Mt. Mitchell.

At 6,684 feet above sea level, North Carolina's Mt. Mitchell is the highest point on land in the United States east of the Mississippi River. I climbed this mountain with Ry, and it was the second high point we did in the year 2023, after finishing Virginia's Mt. Rogers the day before. The Mt. Mitchell Trail is challenging at six miles long with 3,694 feet of elevation gain. Between the 8 miles we did on Mt. Rogers and the 12 miles we did on Mt. Mitchell, we were pretty tired by the end of this one.

Figure 3: Our campsite, near Black Mountain Campground.

Figure 4: Mountain laurels near the beginning of the Mt. Mitchell Trail.

We started the day at our campsite, which we had found near (but not in) Black Mountain Campground (which is where the Mt. Mitchell Trail starts). We had been running late the night before when looking for a campsite, so we still had to drive up to the town of Burnsville, North Carolina, to get food and water for the trail. Once we finally got back and hit the trail, it was about 10:15am. The good ol' Glamper's Start.

Figure 5: A web of tree roots near the beginning of the Mt. Mitchell Trail.

Figure 6: Fungi that look like clams growing on a tree along the Mt. Mitchell Trail.

We saw some places where complex networks of tree roots grew above the ground. We also saw a lot of trees that had fungi which looked like clams growing on them, so we started referring to those fungi as "tree clams". Much like regular clams, these tree clams will work as currency after the nuclear fallout.

Figure 7: The two-mile marker along the Mt. Mitchell Trail.

Figure 8: The 2.5-mile marker along the Mt. Mitchell Trail.

There's a wooden post marking every half mile along the Mt. Mitchell Trail. I got photos of all of them from two miles to the end. It's very important that the existence of these wooden posts is documented on the internet. I'm happy to take care of this for us. I've put two of them here, and the rest of them are down at the bottom of this page.

Figure 9: A small creek crossing, early on in the Mt. Mitchell Trail.

Figure 10: A manmade barrier that unsuccessfully attempts to prevent people from taking a shortcut.

After crossing a small creek, we started to notice some spots where forest debris (stumps, twigs, leaves, etc.) had been intentionally placed, as a deterrent to people taking shortcuts. This was probably just to keep the trail marked, but I find it funny that it's the sort of barrier you'd see in a 1980s role playing video game.

Figure 11: A naturally occurring revolving train station door tree.

Figure 12: An elevated forest view along the Mt. Mitchell Trail.

Next, we came across some trees that look like those revolving metal doors you see at train stations. I tried pushing the tree to rotate it, but it didn't move. Usually, those things only rotate in one direction, so I assumed it must work the other way. Rather than spending too much time there, we continued along the trail.

Figure 13: A campsite awkwardly close to the Mt. Mitchell Trail.

Figure 14: The Great Deku Tree, enjoying its home in North Carolina.

We saw some good campsites from the trail, including one that was awkwardly close to the trail. It was perfect, otherwise. It would make a great place to blast the Baha Men's "Who Let the Dogs Out?" on a Bluetooth speaker at 3am.

Figure 15: The first major clearing along the Mt. Mitchell Trail.

Figure 16: A seventeen-hour smoked brisket tree, just about done.

There's a cool section of the Mt. Mitchell Trail where you switch back several times up the same hill that an artificial clearing is cut out of for power lines. You get views along this section that are progressively higher, and it's a steep hill. Just before we got to this section, we found a smoked brisket tree that looked like it was just about done. It looked pretty good, but I was saving room for either a bear steak or a concession stand hot dog at the summit (more on this further down).

Figure 17: A large, jagged boulder near the summit of Mt. Mitchell.

Figure 18: A flat, overhanging boulder with trees growing out of its ends in a Seussian manner.

When we were near the top of the mountain, we noticed a few huge, wacky-looking boulders. One of them was in a pointy shape that looked like something you'd get with a skew effect in photo editing software. The other one was sort of flat with an overhanging piece that had trees growing out of it.

Figure 19: A tree with many branches.

Figure 20: An empty urn that somebody left along the Mt. Mitchell Trail, near the mountain's summit.

We also found an empty urn (or cookie jar, perhaps?) that somebody left beside the trail. Such an odd thing to put there. It seemed recent, as I would expect any strong storm or wind to knock if over. Maybe the person inside escaped and is roaming the forest?...

Figure 21: A helpful sign identifying some of the mountain's natural features, presumably left behind by previous climbers.

Figure 22: The sign marking the summit of Mt. Mitchell, with the observation platform and the grave of Elisha Mitchell in the background.

Mt. Mitchell is the Mt. Washington of the South. Meaning, it's a large mountain that's tough to climb on foot, but there's also a road that goes right to the top and the summit is adorned with a gift shop, a concession stand, a parking lot, etc. So when you finally make it to the summit, you suddenly run into a bunch of tourists that drove right to the top. On most mountains, the summit is the farthest point from civilization. On Mt. Mitchell, we knew that we'd made it back to civilization when we saw the concession stand sign along the trail. In the disappointment of the century, the stand did not sell hot dogs, or even meat of any sort, real or artificial. Just granola bars and coffee. What is this, some sort of joke? But, other mountains don't offer summit hot dogs, either, so it would have felt like cheating.

Figure 23: A cloudy view from the observation platform at the summit of Mt. Mitchell.

Figure 24: Another cloudy view from the observation platform.

Although we had generally clear weather throughout the day, the summit was the only area that was cloudy, and it was to the point that we had no view of the mountain's surroundings. Even after having visited more than 80% of the country's state high points, I'd say that North Carolina just happened to have the second worst visibility (#1 being Massachussetts) when I visited. I'm sure it's better on some days.
