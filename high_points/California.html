Mt. Whitney (California)

Elevation: 14,505ft / 4,421m
Dates Completed: June 6-7, 2021
Chronology: High point #32 of 50

Figure 1: Mountains of the Sierra Nevada, with Mt. Whitney visible towards the right side of the photo.

Figure 2: Ry and Tom pose at the summit of Mt. Whitney.

With over 6,000 feet of elevation gain, and a round-trip distance of over 22 miles of trail, California's Mt. Whitney was the most difficult mountain I'd climbed so far at the time that I climbed it. Mt. Whitney is the tallest mountain in the contiguous United States, with only certain peaks in Alaska being taller. So far, I consider this my strongest mountaineering achievement.

This was the first state high point that required two days for us to get to. Some people climb the mountain in one day, but doing so is difficult and requires more experience with the mountain than we had. We decided to play it safe and camp at Consultation Lake, about halfway up the trail, on the first night. Neither of us were experienced in backpacking, so our packs were probably a good 10-20 pounds heavier than they could have been.

Figure 3: The Whitney Shelter at the summit of Mt. Whitney.

Figure 4: The first up-close view of the summit, with other successful mountaineers visible.

Whitney's summit is visible for most of the second half of the climb, giving you a constant sense of how far away you are from it. The very last stretch of trail is also gentle--probably one of the easiest parts of the trail, minus the altitude challenges. There's a small shelter at the top, although a sign along the trail warns that the shelter will not protect you from lightning (see Figure 22).

Perhaps the most famous section of the Mt. Whitney Trail is the "99 Switchbacks", which you'll see me continually refer to in quotes because there aren't actually 99 of them, but either 97 or 98 depending on how sharp a 'switchback' is. The point is, the trail climbs a very steep slope up to the top of the ridge by switching back almost a hundred times.

Figure 5: Ry approaches the beginning of the "99 Switchbacks" on the morning of the second day. "The Chute", with barely any snow on it, is also visible.

Figure 6: The Trail Crest, at the top of the "99 Switchbacks", where the trail crosses over onto the ridge.

While ascending the switchbacks, there are times that the drop from one leg of the trail to the next gets steep. Much of the trail is next to some discomforting cliffs. I do think that the switchbacks were the most difficult section of the trail, but I didn't find them as difficult as I was expecting. At the top of the switchbacks, we proceeded to the Trail Crest which leads you across the sloped backside of the ridge.

Next to the 99 Switchbacks is "The Chute", a steep slope that's usually covered in snow, and serves as an alternative path of ascent for times of the year when the switchbacks are under the snow. People also sometimes glissade down the chute to get down the mountain faster (plenty of YouTube videos of this). However, 2021 was an especially light snow year for all of California, so even though it was early June we were able to traverse the whole trail without having to cross any snow.

Figure 7: Consultation Lake in the afternoon.

Figure 8: Consultation Lake, as seen from somewhere around switchback #70.

Ry and I both agreed that Consultation Lake was the coolest place we'd ever camped, as difficult as it was to get to. I liken the effort to carrying a heavy pack up 400 flights of stairs. It was a stop-and-go effort, and when we finally got to the campsite and set up, neither of us did much besides sit and look at the water for a bit, then disappear into our tents for a much needed 10-11 hours of sleep.

Each campsite at Conservation Lake is set on a ledge overlooking the lake. Later on, we practiced bouldering by climbing up and down the ledges to get to and from the lake's edge. There were several large fish in the lake, darting about.

Although there are designated sites to set up camp at along Conservation Lake, I took advantage of a conveniently shaped spot for my tent partially underneath a giant boulder that created a small sort of cave. The spot turned out to be perfect, and I didn't have to deal with much wind at night.

Figure 9: My backpack and tent at the campsite, where I had just set up inside my cave.

Figure 10: Another shot of my tent set up inside the cave.

The front of the ridge is steep--even sheer cliffs at many points including on one side of the Whitney summit. At several points along the trail, there are notches that you can look through and see a drop down of several thousand feet. The notches provided some of my favorite pictures, though I have trouble looking straight down these in person. It's a long, long way down.

The trail on the ridge starts out with more sheer cliffs, only they're even higher than the ones on the 99 Switchbacks. As we proceeded, the cliffs gradually became steep slopes and then less steep slopes, with Mt. Whitney itself being a low enough grade that you could probably scramble up it from that side if you really wanted to, but you'd also have to get to that side of the mountain in the first place which isn't easy.
