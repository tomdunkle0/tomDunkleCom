Eagle Mountain (Minnesota)

Elevation: 2,302ft / 701m
Date Completed: June 2, 2019
Chronology: High point #19 of 50

Figure 1: Eagle Mountain, as viewed from the trail approaching it.

Figure 2: Plaque at Eagle Mountain summit documenting the history of its survey.

Minnesota's Eagle Mountain is surprisingly close to the north shore of Lake Superior. It's not a huge mountain, but it's in the middle of lake-dotted wilderness that's characteristic of Minnesota and pleasant to explore.

Figure 3: Geographical marker at Eagle Mountain.

Figure 4: Tom and Ry pose with a bottle of Woodbridge at the summit of Eagle Mountain.

The summit features a geographic marker and a plaque documenting the history of the mountain's survey. The plaque is engraved in a rock in the ground. The summit is in a wooded area populated by grouse, which make eery sounds.

Figure 5: One of Minnesota's 10,000 lakes, as viewed from the trail to Eagle Mountain.

Figure 6: Footbridges through wetlands along the trail to Eagle Mountain.

The Eagle Mountain Trail passes through lakes and wetlands, and in many places there are wooden footbridges to help you get through the wetter parts. It seemed excessive at times, but I guess in a land like this you just can't always find a dry path between two points.

Also, as was the case in each of the four Great Lakes area states I did on this trip, there were a ton of bugs in the air. The 'black flies' of Minnesota are active this time of year, and the bug nets we wore were useful. Flies are really stupid. They urge to bite your face, but even a loose piece of fabric as coverage prevents them from figuring out how to get to your skin.

Figure 7: The northeastern Minnesota landscape, as viewed from a point close to the summit of Eagle Mountain.

Figure 8: Marshy wetlands characteristic of the area around Eagle Mountain.

Eagle Mountain is only about two miles from the coast of Lake Superior, which demonstrated the calmest water I've ever seen. After we finished climbing the mountain, Ry and I found a 'cart-in' campsite nearby where some wooden carts were left to allow you to bring your camping gear from the parking lot to the lake shore. We found a campsite where there were enough hot embers still burning in the fire pit for us to start a new fire even without a lighter.

Figure 9: A view of Lake Superior along the north shore near Eagle Mountain.

The summit is wooded and not the most scenic part of the trail. The most scenic part is the vantage point from which Figures 10-12 were taken, which was about a quarter of a mile from the summit.
