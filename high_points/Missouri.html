Taum Sauk Mountain (Missouri)

Elevation: 1,772ft / 540m
Date Completed: October 15, 2022
Chronology: High point #38 of 50

Figure 1: The summit of Taum Sauk Mountain--featuring a rock, a plaque, a high-pointer's registry, and a store-brand bench (not an official high pointer's club bench).

Figure 2: Tom, Victoria, and Ry posing at the summit of Taum Sauk Mountain.

Taum Sauk Mountain is an easy high point to bag, but it's not without its rewards. When I left my house in Austin, Texas, to drive towards Taum Sauk Mountain, I told myself that this trip (which was to check off Missouri and Iowa), would likely be the last long road trip I'd do just to grab some easy US high points. The planned "weekend" trip was, and I knew that it would be, an absolute crunch. The driving distance was 2,663 miles, and I drove for a total of 43 hours throughout a five day period. The same period also included two full work days (from two different hotel rooms), both high points, and some stops for barbecue and beer in St. Louis.

Figure 3: Our campsite at the Taum Sauk Mountain Campground, as seen on the morning of our high point visit.

Figure 4: The trail from our campsite to our parking spot at Taum Sauk Mountain Campground.

After picking up Ry and Victoria at the St. Louis airport, we made a quick grocery run and then drove to the Taum Sauk Mountain Campground, which is a maintained campground in Taum Sauk Mountain State Park. The campground is right next to the high point itself, and I joked that this was probably the closest to a high point that we'd ever camped. Although I was expecting generally cold weather for the weekend, it was warm in Missouri--low 60s Fahrenheit, and since I was coming from Texas, I was happy to be camping in a t-shirt all night at that temperature. Since the weather was nice, the campground was almost full, but we managed to claim one of the last sites when we arrived.

Figure 6: A squat shot of Ry on the summit of Taum Sauk Mountain.

The summit of Taum Sauk Mountain is right next to the trailhead for the Mina Sauk Falls trail, which is a three mile loop. We decided to check out the summit first, and hike the trail afterwards. Unsurprisingly, the summit feels more like a clearing in the woods than a mountain peak, but it's peaceful. They've got all of the standard high point fare; a registry, a plaque in the ground, a big rock for squat photos, and a bench that sadly doesn't bear the familiar High Pointer's Foundation logo you find at other points' benches. It's one of those... imposter benches...

Figure 7: A fancy, stand-alone roof at the Mina Sauk Falls Trailhead.

Figure 8: The sign at the Mina Sauk Falls trailhead.

After briefly admiring the state high point, we set off to hike the three-mile-long Mina Sauk Falls trail. As it turns out, mid-October is a good time for leaf-peeping in Missouri, and the last time I got to look at some good leaves that aren't green was when I did the New York and Vermont high points in October back in 2018. We had to compete, because the rules of leaf-peeping clearly state that nobody else is allowed to peep your leaves after you peep them. I've gotten some good photos of red and orange leaves, and I'm posting them here for my own viewing pleasure, but I ask that you kindly refrain from viewing the photos as they're my leaves and I've already peeped them.

As we proceeded along the Mina Sauk Falls trail, we graduated from looking at colorful leaves up close to seeing vibrant, multi-colored hillsides in the distance. First just a glimpse, and eventually more complete views of rolling hills covered in yellow, orange, red, brown and green.

Figure 13: Colorful rolling hills, as seen from the Mina Sauk Falls trail.

Figure 14: Another colorful hill, as seen from the Mina Sauk Falls trail.

I'm generally opposed to digitally zoomed-in photos, but I broke my own rule and took some on the Mina Sauk Falls trail, because I was worried that the colors wouldn't show well from a distance. This is one part of my journal where I recommend looking at the photos on a large computer monitor if you get the chance, and in a separate web browser tab.

Figures 15-18: Several zoomed-in views of colorful, rolling hills from the Mina Sauk Falls trail.

Mina Sauk Falls is a conditional waterfall, and we saw a portion of it along the trail. The waterfall is only active when there's enough rain, and the climate was relatively dry when we visited, so there was very little water flowing. At first, the "waterfall" just looked like a big cluster of boulders in the woods. We climbed down the rocks and eventually found a pool of running water, and some great views. Unfortunately, I only got pictures of the top of the waterfall, since I left my phone behind when we started bouldering to get down.
