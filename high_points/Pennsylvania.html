Mt. Davis (Pennsylvania)

Elevation: 3,213ft / 979m
Date Completed: May 24, 2020
Chronology: High point #24 of 50

Figure 1: The (seemingly) dead woods of southwestern Pennsylvania.

Figure 2: Bill and Ry follow a promising sign.

After pounding beers and eating pizza in the mine fire ghost town of Centralia, we visited the Mt. Davis Natural Area in Pennsylvania to find a spot to set up camp. The forest in this area is one of the strangest forests I've ever been in. Even in late May, everything looks dead. The trees were dotted with some small green leaves, but only just enough to make me question whether they really were dead. Everywhere I looked, fallen trees lined the ground. I wondered whether there was some plague infesting the woods.

Figure 3: A helpful hand demonstrates satisfactory results to the quest to find the geographic marker.

Figure 4: Tom poses in front of the geographic marker at the summit of Mt. Davis.

Figure 5: Ry photographs the geographical marker at Mt. Davis.

The summit of the mountain is below treeline, and the geographic marker for the high point is on top of a pointy boulder at the summit. Nearby are several informational boards on the history of the mountain, as well as an observation tower.

At one point along the trail, we all heard a loud snapping sound from directly above. I looked up, and for about a tenth of a second I thought that a huge bird was taking off from a tree branch above us. Then it occurred to me that there was no bird, and that a large tree branch had just separated from its tree and was falling directly onto us. Ry sidestepped and just barely avoided being hit in the face by this huge tree branch. Based on this encounter, we've all agreed that Pennsylvania is the most dangerous high point we've done.

There was one spot along the trail where we had to stop the listen to two woodpeckers dueling. They were occupying two neighboring trees, and whenever one of them started pecking, the other one felt the need to respond.
