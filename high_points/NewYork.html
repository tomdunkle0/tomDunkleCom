Mt. Marcy (New York)

Elevation: 5,344ft / 1,629m
Date Completed: October 12, 2018
Chronology: High point #10 of 50

Figure 1: Plaque at the summit of Mt. Marcy that explains the history of the mountain's name.

Figure 2: Ry and Bill, approaching the Mt. Marcy summit.

New York's Mt. Marcy is in the heart of the Adirondack Range. It likely wins the award for coldest high point that I've been to so far--given the latitude, the time of year and the fact that we had overcast weather that day.

Figure 3: Tom poses at the summit of Mt. Marcy.

Figure 4: Ry poses at the summit of Mt. Marcy.

It got gradually colder as we proceeded up the mountain. At a certain point, the forest abruptly ended and the wind picked up. It got cold to the point that I was at the absolute low end of my comfort range while dressed in every layer I had with me. The last stretch of the trail from this point wasn't very long, so we quickly hustled up to the summit.

Once we were there, we noticed that the low temperatures had quickly drained the battery on everybody's phone. I was only able to take the few pictures you see here before my phone died. Sadly, the weather was so overcast that we did not get much of a view from the top, anyway. We hung around on the summit only for a little while, then got moving downwards to get back to relative warmth.

Figure 5: A helpful sign marks High Water Bridge, near the beginning of the trail.

Figure 6: Looking up-river from near High Water Bridge.

There's a really interesting river and bridge early on along the Van Hoevenberg trail. The bridge is signed "High Water Bridge", although the water wasn't very high and the river's boulders were more noteworthy than the river itself. It was pretty cool how the low water level and the high boulder level meant that we could freely move back and forth between sides of the river if we wanted.

Figure 7: A clearing below the snowline, with snowy treetops visible above.

Figure 8: The trail to Mt. Marcy, around the beginning of the 'season' line where it became snowy.

There was a distinct 'season line' on the mountain, where once we got above a certain altitude, there was active snowfall but below that the trees and the ground were still green. At one point, the season line was clearly visible even on the same trees (Figure 8).
