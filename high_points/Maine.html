Mt. Katahdin (Maine)

Elevation: 5,269ft / 1,606m
Date Completed: May 26, 2018
Chronology: High point #5 of 50

Figure 1: View of the Knife's Edge with several of central Maine's lakes in the background.

Figure 2: View into the bowl of Katahdin from the Knife's Edge.

Mt. Katahdin is a knee-pounding scramble up large boulders for the first half, and the second half features the famous Knife's Edge, one of my personal favorite sections on any high point trail. The Knife's Edge connects Baxter Peak (the highest peak) to Chimney Peak. There are certain sections of it where the drop is steep and you can certainly get hurt if you're not careful.

The various peaks of Katahdin form a bowl, and as we traversed the Knife's Edge we went clockwise around the bowl. In total, there are three directions that you can reach Baxter Peak from.

Figure 3: Ry and Bill taking a break on top of a large boulder in the pine forest, enduring clouds of mosquitoes.

The area around Baxter State Park boasts many lakes, which also means that it's a humid and buggy environment. We didn't have bug nets for our hats, so at during hours of the day we just had to endure clouds of tiny black flies pelting the face. Luckily, that happened mainly in the evening hours and not during the night. We did also have to put up with it for part of the Helon Taylor Trail, before getting above treeline.

Figures 4-6: Several views of the central Maine landscape, as viewed from the Helon Taylor trail.

Along the Helon Taylor Trail, we got to see a lot of the central Maine landscape once we got above treeline. The weather was sort of cloudy but we never got rained on and it was clear for parts of the day, including while we were at the summit. New England has a way of being unpredictable, so I'm happy that we got what we got.

Figure 7: The Commissioner's plaque at Baxter Peak.

Figure 8: Sign at Baxter Peak pointing directions to various other destinations.

Baxter Peak and Mt. Katahdin are also famous as the northern terminus, or endpoint, of the Appalachian Trail. If you hike the trail south-to-north, it makes for a challenging final section. The sign at Baxter Peak (seen in Figure 8) lists the mileage to Springer Mountain in Georgia, the trail's southern terminus. Although I haven't hiked much of the AT, I've coincidentally been to Springer Mountain because it's very close to where my parents currently live in Georgia.

Figure 9: Panoramic view of Baxter peak, the summit of Mt. Katahdin.

All things considered, I think Mt. Katahdin is my second favorite state high point behind New Mexico, even still as of the current writing (me at 31/50 high points). The mountain's crescent shape is perfect and makes for some really fun trails. The scenery is beautiful, and there's a ton of land to explore.

Figure 10: Many lakes of northern Maine in the distance, as seen from near the summit of Katahdin.

Figure 11: Pine trees along the trail near the treeline.

Katahdin is a very prominent mountain, especially since the surrounding land is flat and dotted with lakes. It's a fun mountain to climb because the lake views change as you gain altitude and you can see a ton of the surroundings. Just across Golden Rd. from Baxter State Park is the Debsconeag Lakes Wilderness Area, which I haven't been to but it apparently has trails of its own.

Figure 12: Panoramic view of our campsite, demonstrating the lavish living conditions provided by a McLean-To.

We camped in a lean-to next to the river at the Roaring Brook Campground, which was a sweet spot. We had to wait several hours to cook dinner the first night since it started raining on our campfire, but the lean-to provided adequate shelter. The lean-to was so nice that we had to define a new class of shelter, called a "McLean-To" to describe it.

After climbing the mountain and joking about calling Maine residents "Maine Courses" and out-of-towners "Side Dishes" all day, we made sure to get several side dishes (canned beans and corn) from the general store outside the park to have with dinner the second night.

Due to the photos' vertical size, I saved these couple for last. But I just wanted to point out a section at the beginning of Knife's Edge, where you have to climb straight down a ~20ft wall and then climb straight back up an equally-sized wall to get onto the edge itself. This wasn't too difficult, but I found it funny that this section is a marked 'trail'.
