King's Peak (Utah)

Elevation: 13,528ft / 4,123m
Dates Completed: July 25-27, 2022
Chronology: High point #37 of 50

Figure 1: View of King's Peak from the east, with the north ridge to Anderson Pass on the right side of the photo.

Figure 2: Tom poses with the sign at the summit of King's Peak.

King's Peak is in the Uinta Mountain range, about twelve miles out from the Henry's Fork trailhead. Because of its remoteness (at least relative to other state high points), climbing King's Peak is a grueling task that most take multiple days to complete. I did so across three days with my brother Bill and our friends Ry and Victoria. In total, we covered approximately 31 miles across 18 hours of hiking.

Figure 3: Ry, Victoria, and Bill crossing the valley towards Dollar Lake.

Figure 4: Victoria and Ry crossing the valley towards Dollar Lake. King's Peak's summit can be seen as a backdrop to closer peaks, slightly left of the photo's center.

On day one, we hit the trail around 4:00pm and carried all of our gear for eight miles to get to Dollar Lake. We established our campsite almost exactly four hours after we'd started, in a small patch of woods a stone's throw from the trail. We all looked forward to leaving most of the gear at the campsite on day two so that we could carry lighter packs. Dollar Lake was our source of water for much of the trip, and when filtered, the lake water was surprisingly good.

The trail goes south through a valley north of King's Peak that looks suspiciously like the overworld of a 1990s role-playing game, and is surrounded by red, blocky, banded peaks with a vague Lego aesthetic. King's Peak is visible from this valley, but just at one specific angle as a backdrop to two closer peaks.

Figure 5: Gunsight Pass (top-left), and the approach to it. Tents can be seen along the trail if you zoom in.

Figure 6: The valley east of King's Peak, as seen from Gunsight Pass.

On the morning of day two, we set out with significantly less weight to carry than the day before, and we finished crossing the valley to get to Gunsight Pass. Gunsight Pass is a channel into another, perhaps even more beautiful valley east of King's Peak. The east valley is also bordered by South King's Peak, and a long, steep scree slope on the opposite side. There are several small lakes in the valley, which we had spectacular views of as we proceeded towards the peak. Near the end of this valley, the trail brought us right up to the edge of King's Peak, so that we could get a good look at the ridge we were about to traverse. It's at this point that you started being able to see other climbers on the ridge if you squint (or, zoom in if you're viewing photos).

Figure 7: The north ridge of King's Peak, as seen from just below Anderson Pass. Other climbers can be seen along the ridge if you zoom in.

Figure 8: The north ridge of King's Peak, as seen from Anderson Pass.

The north ridge of King's Peak begins at Anderson Pass. When we reached Anderson Pass, we were rewarded with our first views of the valley that's west of King's Peak. It's a sharp, crescent-shaped valley that sits peacefully in the protection of Utah's highest point, collecting scree from the surrounding hills.

Interestingly, the whole north ridge seemed to be a haven for butterflies the day we were there. Hundreds of butterflies zipped past me throughout the climb. It was hard to get photos without butterflies bombing them, so every shot you see here is one of several attempts I made at it. You can still see butterflies in some of them (e.g. Figure 15), and they're definitely noticeable in my GoPro footage.

Figures 9-11: Several views of the valley west of King's Peak, as seen from Anderson Pass.

King's Peak doesn't waste any time presenting its flavor of challenge to you. The flavor is one of scrambling up a steep, rocky slope on the left side that abruptly ends at the ridge as a sheer cliff on the right side. It's like this all the way from Anderson Pass up to the mountain's summit. And to be fair, it's not that long of a climb once you're there. The timestamps on my photos show that the process of going Anderson Pass -> Summit -> Anderson Pass only took us about an hour and a half, and that was with a lot of chill time at the summit!

Figure 12: The flat section near the beginning of the north ridge.

Figure 13: A druidic figure stands atop a spire along the north ridge of King's Peak.

Shortly after beginning the climb, you come to a short little flat section that puts things into perspective. That's when you realize just how steep and high the cliff on the right side is. Luckily for us acrophobes, you can keep your distance from the cliff and just climb along the left side, but honestly even that will expose you to some unnerving angles. It's fun, though!

Figure 14: Looking down on a couloir that a couple of other climbers claimed to have descended. Way off in the distance, Dollar Lake is visible.

Figure 15: Bill looking over the edge of the cliff along the north ridge.

There's a couloir that connects Anderson Pass directly to the first valley we hiked across, on day one and the morning of day two. Looking up at it in the morning, it didn't look like a reasonable route of ascent at all. But, we overheard a couple of climbers saying that on a previous trip they had descended via the couloir and saved several hours. Seems compelling, given you could eliminate the east valley from the trip back. But, I'm sure it's sketchy to descend. When looking back at the couloir, we also noticed that we could see Dollar Lake way off in the distance.

Figure 16: Tom manages to get all four group members in one shot.

Figure 17: Bill, Ry, and Victoria at the summit of King's Peak.

At the summit, it was hard not to nap. The views are great, the cliff is still right there next to you, and the physical drain from hiking almost 15 miles uphill starts to set in. To me, what stood for several years as a seemingly unrealistic challenge now felt like a cakewalk. On the other side of having climbed this mountain, it doesn't even seem much more difficult than others I've done. It's the same routine, just with an extra day of hiking on each end. And hiking is just walking.

Figures 18-21: Views from the summit of King's Peak.

I think we chose the right days to climb, since we had perfect weather and the trail wasn't crowded. On the way down on day three, we passed several groups of more than 40 people each climbing up together, making it look like the ridge and summit would likely be crowded the next day. At one point, a huge group passed us and was followed a minute later by an individual guy leading a pack of five white llamas tied together and each carrying several packs. We figured that guy and the llamas were hauling supplies for the group.
