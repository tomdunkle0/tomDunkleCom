Mt. Rogers (Virginia)

Elevation: 5,730ft / 1,750m
Date Completed: May 18, 2023
Chronology: High point #40 of 50

Figure 1: Tom's foot at the geographic marker on the summit of Mt. Rogers.

Figure 2: Tom and Ry posing in ultra high visibility layers at the summit of Mt. Rogers.

Mt. Rogers was the first US state high point that I climbed in the year 2023. It's located in western Virginia, close to the border with North Carolina. I climbed the mountain with Ry, and it took us about five and a half hours round trip. We started from the Elk Garden Trailhead, which is about four miles from the summit along the Appalachian Trail.

Figure 3: The beginning of the Mt. Rogers portion of the Appalachian Trail, as seen from the Elk Garden trailhead parking lot.

Figure 4: The parking lot at the Elk Garden Trailhead, as seen from slightly up the hill from the trailhead.

The trail begins in a picturesque, rolling hillside reminiscent of the default Windows XP desktop background. A bench sits atop one of the hills, providing a view of the valley below. Wildflowers give the hills a golden color.

Figure 5: A bench overlooking the valley near the Elk Garden Trailhead.

Figure 6: Wildflowers near the beginning of the trail.

After only about 10 to 15 minutes, the trail becomes forested for the rest of the trek. Brief clearings offer a view of a nearby hill. Perhaps a hill of beans? And, at one point, we saw a tree whose roots appeared to be consuming a boulder. We joked about early settlers eating bear steaks, and we kept our eyes out hoping to bring home some steaks of our own. Just kidding. Hunting black bears is only legal in 27 states and Virginia isn't one of them. Nothing to see in this paragraph, folks--keep scrolling.

Figure 7: A tree whose roots appear to be consuming a boulder.

Figure 8: A large hill.

After hiking for an hour, we got the first glimpses of the shape of Mt. Rogers, which could be seen through the trees. This whole section of the Appalachian Trail is on Mt. Rogers (i.e. we didn't cross any foothills first), so it was validating to see (what was probably) the summit at this point on the way up.

Figure 9: The first view of Mt. Rogers, through the trees along the Appalachian Trail.

Figure 10: Ry posing with Mt. Rogers in the background, at the point where we could first see the mountain through the trees.

Around the halfway point is an intersection with a horse trail and a spring. Here, we can see the difference in attitudes towards horses, where Virginia posts signs to welcome horses to its spring while Wisconsin strictly prohibits horses from its swimming beach. Intriguing!

Figure 11: A sign along the Appalachian Trail marking two miles from the Mt. Rogers Spur trail. Horses are implicitly warmly welcomed to a nearby spring.

Figure 12: Appalachian mountains visible through the trees along the Appalachian Trail.

An hour and a half in, we came to a large clearing that provided the best views we got for the whole day. We could see some of the foothills and valleys surrounding Mt. Rogers.

Figure 13: Looking to the left from a vantage point along the Mt. Rogers section of the Appalachian Trail.

Figure 14: Looking straight ahead from the same vantage point.

There was a murmur of human voices in the distance, but we couldn't tell where from. After walking for a few minutes, we came upon another clearing from which we could hear the voices better, but still with difficulty. It seemed like they were shouting, but we couldn't tell if they were angry or excited shouts. It was hard to read their tone. It must have been a couple of restless ghosts arguing in the hills.

Figure 15: Looking to the right from the vantage point.

Figure 16: Another view of the same clearing from which an argument between bickering ghosts could be heard.

After we escaped the ghosts, there was another short, open section where we got good views of the surrounding hills and valleys. Just before the Appalachian Trail reaches the Thomas Knob Shelter, there's a connection to the Mt. Rogers Spur Trail. The Spur Trail is a short offshoot that goes to the Mt. Rogers summit.

Figure 17: An Appalachian valley above the treetops, near the Mt. Rogers summit.

Figure 18: Signs at the beginning of the Mt. Rogers Spur Trail.

The Mt. Rogers Spur Trail led us through a dark and mossy forest, teeming with color, but not sunlight. Struck by the beauty and remoteness of the place, we decided to name it Bawl's Deep National Forest, in honor of a fictitious character, Alfred Bawl. His backstory isn't important.

Figure 19: A car air freshener forest found in Bawl's Deep National Forest, Virginia.

Figure 21: Tall, mossy pine trees found in Bawl's Deep National Forest, Virginia.

Figure 22: Thick moss covering the fallen trees in Bawl's Deep National Forest, Virginia.

On the other side of Bawl's Deep National Forest is the summit of Mt. Rogers, a forested point with several boulders, the largest of which features a geographic marker. When we got here, I was standing at my 40th state high point. Having completed 80% of the country's state high points, I appreciated the fact that I'd finally become a grade B- high pointer.
