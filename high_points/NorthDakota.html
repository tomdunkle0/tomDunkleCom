White Butte (North Dakota)

Elevation: 3,506ft / 1,069m
Date Completed: September 23, 2021
Chronology: High point #34 of 50

Figure 1: The hills along the trailhead demonstrate a chalky, white stripe. This view is from about halfway up, looking back towards the trailhead.

Figure 2: Tom poses with the military box at the summit of White Butte.

North Dakota's high point, White Butte, has both one of the funniest and most accurate names of any high point. White Butte is made of light grey and white soil and juts out of flat farmland in southwestern North Dakota. The rest of the landscape is somewhere between the amber waves and an ominous inland aesthetic. It's like being in the Great Plains, but as we joked it felt like we might get attacked by bandits. We did not.

Figures 3-5: Several views of North Dakota from the summit of White Butte.

I visited the butte with my friends Ry and Aaron. We had just high-pointed South Dakota, and although we still wanted to do other things there, we spent the afternoon and evening of one day driving up to North Dakota just for its high point, and dinner. It took about three hours each way to cover the distance, plus a stop on the way up to pick up some liquor for the coming night. We got to the butte with no time to spare, as the whole process of summitting and returning took about an hour, and it was the last hour of daylight that we had. But we did it, and got some good photos. Upon arriving at the parking lot, we immediately found some cool views of certain parts of the butte.

Figures 6-9: Views of parts of White Butte just adjacent to the trailhead.

Several hundred feet from the parking lot was a herd of black cows. I took some pictures of them from a distance, and after we started climbing the butte, I noticed that whenever we looked back the cows seemed to be closer to the parking lot. They must have been looking out of curiosity to see what kind of car Aaron drives.

Figure 10: A herd of black cows, as seen from the parking lot a few hundred feet away.

Figure 11: A distant view of the entrance to White Butte, along White Butte trail. If you zoom in on the parking lot at the end of the road, just to the right you can see the herd of cows.

After a few minutes on the trail, there's a fork where you're supposed to take a left and go up the hill, but we initially took a right and found a dead end in a valley surrounded by the largest walls the butte has to offer. The walls are detailed, featured, and a bright white color. It looks like it would be fun to climb straight up, but this is a delicate area on private land, so we didn't try.

Figure 12: A view of the more 'technical' climb up through the valley of White Butte (ill-advised).

Figure 13: Another view of the valley of White Butte.

After we turned around and went the correct way, the trail brought us up a somewhat Tolkeinian hill that I would say was the steepest part of the hike. The sign at the trailhead had warned us of rattlesnakes. It was too cold out when we visited for snakes to have been hanging out on the surface, but I figured that this hill is where they'd be the most dangerous if you did encounter one (side note--I've been rattled at by a rattlesnake near my home in central Texas before. Kind of cool, but scary as hell). At the top of the hill, the trail follows the ridgeline up to the summit.

Figure 14: Perhaps the steepest section of the trail, about halfway up.

Figure 15: Ry approaches the ridgeline section of the trail.

There's a small forest hidden on the side of the butte opposite the parking lot, i.e. you don't see the forest until you're close enough to the top to see over the ridge. Since we visited in late September, the forest was in the process of changing colors. After arriving at the summit, we went down to a lower ledge to drink our summit beers and get photos of the North Dakotan sunset over the forest.
