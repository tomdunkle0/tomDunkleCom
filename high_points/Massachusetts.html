Mt. Greylock (Massachusetts)

Elevation: 3,849ft / 1,063m
Date Completed: November 25, 2016
Chronology: High point #3 of 50

Figure 1: The tower at the Mt. Greylock summit.

Massachusetts had received a snowstorm a couple of days before we climbed Mt. Greylock. The climb itself happened in nice, clear weather, but we had to do it in deep snow which slowed us down a bit. Perhaps the least favorable element of the day was the New England sunset, which happens as early as 4:00pm in the winter. We did end up coming part of the way down in the dark.

At the summit is a large tower that looks like a chess pawn. Although most of the trail was in clear conditions, the top of the mountain was extremely foggy. I captured one cool video of the tower slowly fading into view as I walked closer to it.

Figure 2: A view of the sky from the Mt. Greylock trail.

Figure 3: Evergreen trees near the Mt. Greylock summit.

The rest of the summit was a snowy field dotted with evergreens, and one side open for a view that we couldn't see much of due to the fog. Obviously, I'd rank this mountain relatively low on the difficulty scale of US high points, but it packs a surprising punch when you have to climb it in deep snow. Maybe it's good practice for that same sort of feature on a more serious mountain like Mt. Hood or Mt. Rainier.

In the days between the snowstorm and our group climbing the mountain, a few others had also made the trek. This made the trail easier to follow, as slushy footprints lined it all the way up. For a good seasonal contrast of the northeast, look at my photos of the Connecticut high point(s) next. They are very close to Mt. Greylock, but I did them in the summer.

On this trip I was joined by my friends Randall and Lars, in addition to the usual suspects. They're the two leftmost people in the panorama at the top of the page. We saw a couple of other people along the trail, both locals, but otherwise there was not a lot of human activity. Just a bit too dreary and snowy of a day for most people, I guess.

The snowy woods of New England are calm and serene, but there isn't much to talk about besides trees, snow, and clouds. So, I'll point out some interesting trees. There's a bowed tree that's almost fallen over (Figure 8), a stripped tree that may have been hit by lightning (Figure 9), and a fallen tree (Figure 10). And if you search the photos hard enough, you'll find many more trees.
