Humphreys Peak (Arizona)

Elevation: 12,637ft / 3,852m
Date Completed: June 6, 2020
Chronology: High point #29 of 50

Figure 1: View of Humphreys Peak from our campsite in Sunset Crater Volcano National Monument.

Figure 2: View of north central Arizona from the summit of Humphreys Peak.

At the time that I completed Humphreys Peak, I did not consider it the most difficult high point I'd gotten to yet (New Mexico, Maine, and Colorado were all more difficult overall). But, due to the circumstances I considered the very last portion of Humphreys to be the most difficult section I'd crossed so far to get to a state high point. In some ways, I consider the summit of Humphreys to be the moment in my life when I became a true mountaineer.

Figure 3: Ridgeline trail approaching the summit of Humphreys Peak.

Figure 4: Snowy valley and neighboring peaks, as viewed from near the summit of Humphreys Peak.

We took the Humphreys Peak Trail, with the trailhead starting at the parking lot for the Arizona Snowbowl. The trail meets with Doyle Saddle shortly before the summit of the mountain. Several people along the trail had warned us of very strong winds and low temps at the summit. As one man specifically claimed: "70mph winds and sub-zero temperatures". There is debate among the group as to how true that claim is.

Just after taking a left to continue on the trail at the intersection, we hiked along a gentle ridge, not finding the trail itself daunting, but as we continued the harsh winds from the left steadily increased. Then, after emerging from one final passage with a bit of leftward protection in the form of boulders, it happened. Suddenly, we were being blasted with winds that felt about twice as strong as before, and it became clear that standing upright was going to be difficult if not impossible during the final stretch.

Figure 5: Several pairs of feet at the summit of Humphreys Peak.

Figure 6: Tom poses at the summit of Humphreys Peak, with a wall of rocks behind him protecting him from ~70mph winds.

We straddled the side of the mountain, intentionally crawling around the side that would keep us shielded from the wind. Clutching large boulders one at a time, I sidled up to the summit. At the summit, a large rock wall had previously been constructed to shield climbers from the wind. On the unobstructed side was one of the most spectacular views I've ever taken in. Perhaps I was delerious from having defeated the wind.

On my way back down the windy section, I was ballsy enough to just stand up straight and run through the most exposed and windiest part. It felt like running while submerged in water, and somehow I was able to do it. As soon as we all got out of the windy part, we sat on a protected ledge and took a breather while Aaron played music on his backpack speakers. We knew that we were in the clear at that point.

Figure 7: Looking down on the remnants of a prior avalanche from a switchback on the Humphreys Peak trail.

Figure 8: Looking up at the same avalanche from the same switchback on the Humphreys Peak trail.

At a couple of points along the trail, we saw the remains of avalanches. One of the trail's switchbacks is right along an avalanche line, and Figures 7 and 8 demonstrate looking down and up from that switchback. Naturally, I thought about how fun it would be to just climb up the avalanche line, but I wasn't sure that it would go anywhere useful. The avalanche lines are similar to the ones visible on Wheeler Peak in New Mexico.

Another cool feature was the snow spiral that was visible inside the bowl of Humphreys Peak. Presumably the wind currents tend to a circular direction because of the bowl which causes snow to drift into the spiral pattern.

Our campsite was also fantastic on this trip. It was in the Sunset Crater Volcano National Monument. The first thing we did when we got there was get my car struck in loose volcanic gravel, but the guys all jumped out and pushed and they were able to help me get it out.

Then, we found this sweet secluded spot up on top of a hill, with a perfect view of the mountain. There was also a tree that we hung our eating utensils from. After we set up camp, we wandered around the area surveying our own holes for a round of disc golf, and occasionally cheering at dirt bikers and ATVers riding around the area.
