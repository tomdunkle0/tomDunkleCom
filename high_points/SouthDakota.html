Black Elk Peak (South Dakota)

Elevation: 7,231ft / 2,204m
Date Completed: September 22, 2021
Chronology: High point #33 of 50

Figure 1: The Black Hills of South Dakota, with the tower of Black Elk Peak on the horizon, about one-third left of center of the shot.

Figure 2: Tom, Aaron, and Ry pose on the tower at the summit of Black Elk Peak.

From my home in Austin, Texas, I took a trip all the way up north to check off the high points of North and South Dakota, with a detour over to Fraser, Colorado on the way to meet up with my friends Ry and Aaron. At the time, the Dakotas were two of the small handful of states I'd never even been to before.

After driving seven hours from Fraser, through Wyoming and into South Dakota, we parked at the Black Elk Peak trailhead in Custer State Park. In jest, I noted that Custer (and state parks in general) are cool and all, but I prefer it when state high points are not in the middle of them.

Figure 3: Flags arranged near the summit of Black Elk Peak.

Figure 4: Looking east across the Black Hills, with the preserved side of Mt. Rushmore in the distance.

The Black Hills are a unique landscape. The rocks are both pointy and smooth, depending on how close you're viewing them from. They're no Rocky Mountains, but the hills contrast with the mostly flat landscape in the surrounding areas, and they present tons of opportunity for rock climbers. Visiting here made me think about getting more serious with rope climbing. More on that later.

The trail is about three miles long, and it's mostly horizontal. There's an arc in the trail, and we were joking that it would be faster to just run down into the valley and cut across. Looking back at the map now, that definitely would not have saved time, though I'm sure it would have been fun.

Figure 9: Another hill rising from the forest.

Figure 10: A large rock face, seen from the Black Elk Peak trail.

The summit of Black Elk Peak is a tall rock spire, similar to other rocks in the area, but there is an observation tower constructed on top of it. Without the tower, I suspect it'd still be easily climbable, but there's a manmade walkway and staircase that leads you up through the inside of the rock, which makes the approach a bit more exciting. At the top of the stairs, just before entering the tower is a plaque indicating the mountain's height and pointing out that it's the highest point east of the Rocky Mountains and west of Europe. The plaque also illustrates that the mountain used to be called Harney Peak, before it was renamed in 2016.

Figure 11: The plaque at the summit of Black Elk Peak.

Figure 12: Tom poses in front of the plaque on Black Elk Peak.

Inside the observation tower is a lofted deck that you can climb up to and see out the windows. It's a nifty little feature, but the views are cooler if you just stand on the deck outside the building. The stairs to get up to the loft are also steep and sketchy.

Figures 13-16: Several views from inside the observation tower on top of Black Elk Peak.

The stairwell in the tower also goes down a level, into an eery basement that has an alternate door leading outside. If you go through that door, a path leads you down to a small lake hidden on the side of the hill, with a concrete dam. Next to the lake is another small watchtower that you can go inside (or on top of). It has a window that looks out over the lake.

Figure 17: Aaron and Ry investigate the tower overlooking the dammed lake.

Figure 18: The lake just below the summit of Black Elk Peak, maintained by a concrete dam.

From the lake, each of us climbed in a different direction up the surrounding rocks. From where I stood, I took the pictures in Figures 19-21 and made a comic strip of Ry and Aaron climbing the watchtower. It's hard to see them in the last frame, since they and the tower are in the shadows.

Figures 19-21: Aaron and Ry find a way on top of the lower watchtower.

About 300 feet south of Black Elk Peak is a steep peg of a mountain called McGillicuddy's Peak. Clearly one of the more technical summits in South Dakota, it's steep enough to require rope climbing (skills I do not possess yet).

As we stood on the tower on Black Elk peak, I started fearing that maybe McGillicuddy's is actually the taller of the two at its natural point, and that maybe people just got lazy and built the tower on the easier peak to reach. But no, when I looked it up later I learned that the latest measurements show McGillicuddy's is less than two feet shorter than Black Elk Peak, at their highest natural points.

Highpointers everywhere dodged a bullet on this one. South Dakota may end up being one of the most technical climbs if two feet of Black Elk Peak ever get shaved off in a storm. On the way down, Ry and I took a detour over to the base of McGillicuddy's to get a closer look and confirm that there was no way in hell we could climb it.
